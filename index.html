The Changing Path to Presidency

How the ideal presidential profile has changed across U.S. history

By: Carolyn Cullen, Meghan Levitt, and Erik Martin Published: April 2026

"I walk on untrodden ground. There is scarcely any action, whose motives may not be subject to a double interpretation. There is scarcely any part of my conduct which may not hereafter be drawn into precedent." - George Washington, in a letter to Catherine Macaulay Graham, 1790 [1] .

When George Washington wrote that he was walking on “untrodden ground,” the idea of what a president should look like was still unknown. There was no established model for what kind of background would prepare someone for the role, and every decision Washington made carried the weight of precedent.

More than two centuries later, the presidency is no longer new. Every four years, the American people vote to elect our next president. We have had 47 presidents in our 250 year history, and each of them have faced unique challenges as the leader of our country. The backgrounds of these presidents have varied, while having a few key things in common. For example, the almost 50 year period of the Cold War saw the election of presidents that all had some form of military experience.

The first pattern is hard to miss: every president elected during the Cold War and Civil War had some form of military experience. Compared with the modern and world wars era, where military service is much less common, this suggests that voters once associated military background with national readiness in a way they may not today.

The Cold War, a standstill between the United States and the Soviet Union (USSR), along with their allies, saw the lack of outright conflict between the two nations due to the fear of mutually assured destruction with the usage of a nuclear weapon. The fear Americans experienced as a result of tensions with the Soviet Union was exacerbated by Senator Joseph McCarthy's campaign against alleged Communist influence in the United States [2]. The American people wanted a president who would be able to lead them during this difficult time, and the name that came up was Dwight D. Eisenhower, a five star general. Thanks to his service in World War II, he was identified as a candidate who was up to the task [3]. Americans voted overwhelmingly to elect Eisenhower during the time that is commonly considered the peak of the Cold War [4], and he soon signed an armistice to bring America's three year participation in the Korean War to an end.

Eisenhower was elected a second time in 1956, and was followed by Senator John F. Kennedy, who vowed to progress the country into the future [5]. While the unease experienced by the United States continued and the Cold War did not formally end until 1991, the presidents elected during this time reflect the way Americans wanted their country to be led.

Figure 3. Presidents' military experience levels across time.

Each dot represents the start and end of a term for one president and is placed according to the highest level of military experience recorded for that president. Hover over a dot to view more information about the president.

Note: If a president had more than one military title, the highest was used for the sake of this visualization. Each military title was hand-sorted into the five levels, as defined below:
  • Top command includes titles with: "commander-in-chief" or "general of the army"
  • Senior leadership includes titles with: "major general", "brigadier general" or "brigadier gen"
  • Mid-level officer includes titles with: "colonel", "major", "commander -" or "quartermaster general"
  • Lower-level service includes titles with: "captain", "lieutenant", "lt. commander" or "private"
  • No service: no title or experience listed

Viewed this way, the Cold War stands out as a period when military experience remained consistently present, even as the type of experience varied. Eisenhower represents the high point of military command, but later Cold War presidents show that military service could still matter symbolically even when it was not the defining feature of a president's career.

Beyond military service, presidential backgrounds reveal other patterns that become clearer when viewed by era. These factors do not tell us exactly what every voter wanted, but they do show how the presidency has been shaped by different kinds of experience, authority, and personal background over time. Military service may have mattered most during periods of war and international tension, but other traits, such as family background and use of presidential power, also help show how the profile of the “typical” president has changed. One especially striking example is executive orders: presidents serving during the World Wars era signed far more executive orders than presidents in any other period, reflecting how moments of national crisis can expand the demands and powers of the presidency.

The Founding Generation, which we mark as 1789 (the election of George Washington) to 1861 (just before the start of the Civil War), was made up of fifteen men. This time, as Washington wrote, helped set a precedent for future presidents to come. The first five presidents were founding fathers, with John Quincy Adams being the first president that was not a founding father. However, during this time, only white male citizens were allowed to vote [6], so only their choices are representative of what the nation wanted in a leader.

The Civil War & Reconstruction era was the next major period in American history. The South seceded from the North to form the Confederate States of America due to tensions around slavery, then later reintegrated after their surrender. The war lasted from 1861 to 1865, with the Reconstruction period following until 1877. This time also marked the beginning of industrialization, with the invention of newspapers and the telegraph [7]. In 1870, the right to vote was extended to Black men, further widening who got to choose the next president, although this did not guarantee a proportionate say in who was elected. All presidents during this time had some form of military experience.

Industrialization picked up steam from 1877 to 1916. This marked a time of innovation for the United States as urbanization grew and workers rights were not yet protected. Presidents of this era had to face monopolies, an influx of immigrants, and workers rights [8].

America joined World War I in 1917 and left combat in 1919 with the Treaty of Versailles [9]. This period led into the roaring twenties and later, the Great Depression. America then joined World War II in 1941 and fought until its end in 1945 [10]. The country endured much tumult during this period, and the presidents elected were elected to navigate this time for them.

The Cold War era, from 1946 to 1991, continued the emphasis on national security, but in a different form. Rather than a single declared war, this period was shaped by long-term tension between the United States and the Soviet Union. As shown earlier, every president elected during this era had some form of military experience.

The modern period, from 1992 to present, has seen presidents with mostly legal or business backgrounds prior to their presidencies, with all of them holding public office prior to their election except for one, Donald J. Trump. This time period includes conflicts in the Middle East, a push for more rights for LGBTQ+ individuals, and an intense shift in how we do politics.

While era averages help reveal the overall historical patterns, they can also hide the presidents who do not fit neatly into those patterns. Not every president matched the dominant profile of his time, and some presidents stand out precisely because their background differed from the era around them. Looking at individual presidents allows us to move from the “typical” president to the more complicated reality of presidential careers.

In examining the individual radar charts of presidents in comparison to their era's averages, one can gain insights into their distinctiveness for the time, or lack thereof. Further, there are interesting similarities that can be drawn through comparisons of individual presidents' radar charts. Unsuspecting presidents can exhibit very similar profiles. One example of this is William McKinley and Harry Truman. Despite representing different parties, the radar chart profiles of these two presidents are virtually indistinguishable.

The presidency began with uncertainty, as Washington's words suggest, but over time the office developed expectations shaped by war, expansion, crisis, social change, and political media. Each era produced a different image of readiness: the Founding Generation emphasized precedent and elite political leadership, the Civil War and World Wars elevated crisis leadership; the Cold War kept military service visible as a sign of national security experience, and the modern era has increasingly reflected a whole new type of presidency.

The shift away from military experience is especially important. Earlier presidents often gained public authority through service in war or military command, while more recent presidents have usually reached the office through other paths. This does not mean military experience has disappeared from American politics, or that it no longer matters to voters. Rather, it suggests that the background associated with presidential readiness has changed. As war, communication, campaigning, and governing have evolved, so have the traits that appear most closely tied to presidential leadership.
