This is a short framing paragraph. Use it to ground the reader before they encounter the first visualization. Think of it like a transition between the big headline and the actual reporting.

Start with the main tension or question

This opening section should read like a professional feature story. Introduce the issue, give enough context for a general audience, and signal what the reader should pay attention to. Keep paragraphs on the shorter side so the page feels readable and paced.

You can also use this area to briefly explain the source of your data, the scope of the analysis, and why the story is worth telling. Avoid dumping methods all at once. Let the structure of the page do some of that explanatory work for you.

Interpret what the reader just saw

After each chart, return to prose. This is where the article starts to feel more like a news feature and less like a dashboard. Tell the reader what matters in the interaction they just explored.

Good narrative structure often alternates between explanation, evidence, and interpretation. That rhythm helps the piece feel guided instead of overwhelming.

“A strong pull quote can break up the layout, emphasize your central takeaway, and make the page feel more editorial.” Optional: Interview excerpt, synthesis, or author takeaway

End with a clear takeaway

Your conclusion should not just repeat the headline. It should leave the reader with a sharper understanding of the pattern, why it matters, and what the evidence suggests.

For a class project, this is also a good place to briefly note limits in the data or analysis while still ending with confidence.
