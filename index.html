The Changing Path to Presidency

How the ideal presidential profile has changed across U.S. history

By: Carolyn Cullen, Meghan Levitt, and Erik Martin Published: April 2026

"I walk on untrodden ground. There is scarcely any action, whose motives may not be subject to a double interpretation. There is scarcely any part of my conduct which may not hereafter be drawn into precedent." - George Washington, in a letter to Catherine Macaulay Graham, 1790

When George Washington wrote that he was walking on “untrodden ground,” the idea of what a president should look like was still unknown. There was no established model for what kind of background would prepare someone for the role, and every decision Washington made carried the weight of precedent.

Note: If a president had more than one military title, the highest was used for the sake of this visualization. Each military title was hand-sorted into the five levels, as defined below:
  • Top command includes titles with: "commander-in-chief" or "general of the army"
  • Senior leadership includes titles with: "major general", "brigadier general" or "brigadier gen"
  • Mid-level officer includes titles with: "colonel", "major", "commander -" or "quartermaster general"
  • Lower-level service includes titles with: "captain", "lieutenant", "lt. commander" or "private"
  • No service: no title or experience listed
